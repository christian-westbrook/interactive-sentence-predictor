&lt;!-- Definition and uses --&gt;
'''Ampicillin''' is an [[antibiotic]] used to prevent and treat a number of [[bacterial infection]]s, such as [[respiratory tract infection]]s, [[urinary tract infections]], [[meningitis]], [[salmonellosis]], and [[endocarditis]].&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt; It may also be used to prevent [[group B streptococcal infection]] in newborns.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt; It is used by mouth, by [[intramuscular injection|injection into a muscle]], or intravenously.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015&gt;{{cite web|title=Ampicillin|url=http://www.drugs.com/monograph/ampicillin.html|publisher=The American Society of Health-System Pharmacists|accessdate=1 August 2015|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20150712001731/http://www.drugs.com/monograph/ampicillin.html|archivedate=12 July 2015|df=dmy-all}}&lt;/ref&gt; Like all antibiotics, it is not useful for the treatment of viral infections.

&lt;!-- Adverse effects --&gt;
Common side effects include rash, nausea, and diarrhea.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt; It should not be used in people who are [[allergic to penicillin]].&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt; Serious side effects may include [[Clostridium difficile colitis|''Clostridium difficile'' colitis]] or [[anaphylaxis]].&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt; While usable in those with [[kidney problem]]s, the dose may need to be decreased.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt; Its use during [[pregnancy]] and [[breastfeeding]] appears to be generally safe.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt;&lt;ref&gt;{{cite web|title=Ampicillin use while Breastfeeding|url=http://www.drugs.com/breastfeeding/ampicillin.html|accessdate=1 August 2015|date=March 2015|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20150923220701/http://www.drugs.com/breastfeeding/ampicillin.html|archivedate=23 September 2015|df=dmy-all}}&lt;/ref&gt;

In the United States, it is available as a [[generic medication]] and 10 days of treatment cost about $13.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt;

==Medical uses==
Ampicillin is used to treat infections by many [[Gram-positive bacteria|Gram-positive]] and [[Gram-negative bacteria]].  Ampicillin was the first 'broad spectrum' penicillin with activity against Gram-positive bacteria including ''[[Streptococcus pneumoniae]]'', ''[[Streptococcus pyogenes]]'', some isolates of ''[[Staphylococcus aureus]]'' (but not penicillin-resistant or methicillin-resistant strains), and some ''[[Enterococcus]]''. Activity against Gram-negative bacteria includes ''[[Neisseria meningitidis]]'', some ''[[Haemophilus influenzae]]'', and some of the [[Enterobacteriaceae]]. Its spectrum of activity is enhanced by co-administration of [[sulbactam]], a drug that inhibits [[beta lactamase]], an enzyme produced by bacteria to inactivate ampicillin and related antibiotics.&lt;ref name =Hauser&gt;Hauser AR (2013). ''Antibiotic Basics for Clinicians'', 2nd ed. Lippincott, Williams, and Wikins, pp. 25–28&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;{{cite journal |author=Akova M |title=Sulbactam-containing beta-lactamase inhibitor combinations |journal=Clin. Microbiol. Infect. |volume=14 Suppl 1 |issue= |pages=185–8 |date=January 2008 |pmid=18154545 |doi=10.1111/j.1469-0691.2007.01847.x |url=}}&lt;/ref&gt; It is sometimes used in combination with other antibiotics that have different mechanisms of action, like [[vancomycin]], [[linezolid]], [[daptomycin]], and [[tigecycline]].&lt;ref name=Suleyman2016rev&gt;{{cite journal|last1=Suleyman|first1=G|last2=Zervos|first2=MJ|title=Safety and efficacy of commonly used antimicrobial agents in the treatment of enterococcal infections: a review.|journal=Expert opinion on drug safety|date=2016|volume=15|issue=2|pages=153-67|doi=10.1517/14740338.2016.1127349|pmid=26629598}}&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;{{cite book|last1=Torok|first1=M. Estee|last2=Moran|first2=Ed|last3=Cooke|first3=Fiona|title=Oxford Handbook of Infectious Diseases and Microbiology|publisher=Oxford University Press|isbn=9780191503108|page=721|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=G4KuDQAAQBAJ&amp;pg=PA721|language=en|deadurl=no|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20170323064830/https://books.google.com/books?id=G4KuDQAAQBAJ&amp;pg=PA721|archivedate=23 March 2017|df=dmy-all}}&lt;/ref&gt;

Ampicillin can be administered by [[oral administration |mouth]], an [[intramuscular injection]] (shot) or by [[intravenous infusion]].&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt;

==Side effects==
Ampicillin is comparatively less toxic than other antibiotics. In very rare cases, it causes severe side effects such as [[angioedema]], [[anaphylaxis]], and ''C. difficile'' infection. Some develop [[Black hairy tongue|black 'furry' tongue']]. The most serious adverse effects are [[seizure]]s, [[serum sickness]], [[anaphylaxis]], [[pseudomembranous colitis]]. The most common side effects to be expected in ten percent of users are [[diarrhea]] and [[rash]]. Less common side effects can be [[nausea]], [[vomiting]], [[itching]], blood [[dyscrasia#Modern use|dyscrasia]]s, and mild [[allergic reactions]]. Other conditions may develop up several weeks after treatment.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt;&lt;ref name=Davis&gt; {{cite web| url = http://davisplus.fadavis.com/| access-date = March 22, 2017| publisher = Davis| title = Ampicillin| date = 2017| deadurl = no| archiveurl = https://web.archive.org/web/20170225031940/http://davisplus.fadavis.com/| archivedate = 25 February 2017| df = dmy-all}}&lt;/ref&gt;

==Interactions==
Ampicillin reacts with [[probenecid]] to decrease [[renal excretion]]. Large doses of ampicillin can increase the risk of bleeding with concurrent use of [[warfarin]]. Ampicillin use can make [[oral contraceptives]] less effective.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt;&lt;ref name = Davis/&gt;

==Mechanisms==
Ampicillin is in the [[penicillin]] group of [[β-Lactam antibiotic|beta-lactam antibiotic]]s and is part of the [[aminopenicillin]] family. It is roughly equivalent to [[amoxicillin]] in terms of activity.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt;

Ampicillin is able to penetrate Gram-positive and some Gram-negative bacteria. It differs from [[Benzylpenicillin|penicillin G]], or benzylpenicillin, only by the presence of an [[amino]] group. That amino group helps the drug penetrate the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria.{{citation needed|date=March 2017}}

Ampicillin acts as an irreversible inhibitor of the enzyme [[DD-transpeptidase|transpeptidase]], which is needed by bacteria to make the cell wall.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt; It inhibits the third and final stage of bacterial cell wall synthesis in [[binary fission]], which ultimately leads to cell [[lysis]]; therefore, ampicillin is usually bacteriolytic.&lt;ref name=AHFS2015/&gt;&lt;ref name = &quot;Goodman&quot;&gt;Petri WA in Brunton LL, Chabner BA, Knollmann BC (eds.) (2011 ) ''Goodman and Gilman's The Pharmacological Basis of Therapeutics'', 12th ed., Chapter 53. McGraw-Hill, New York.&lt;/ref&gt;

==History==
Ampicillin has been used extensively to treat bacterial infections since 1961.&lt;ref name = acred&gt;{{cite journal |title=Pharmacology and chemotherapy of ampicillin--a new broad-spectrum penicillin |journal=Br J Pharmacol Chemother |volume=18 |issue= 2|pages=356–69 |date=April 1962 |pmid=13859205 |pmc=1482127 |doi= 10.1111/j.1476-5381.1962.tb01416.x|url=|author1=Acred |first1=P |last2=Brown |first2=D. M. |last3=Turner |first3=D. H. |last4=Wilson |first4=M. J. }}&lt;/ref&gt; Until the introduction of ampicillin by the British company [[Beecham (pharmaceutical company)|Beecham]], penicillin therapies had only been effective against Gram-positive organisms such as [[Staphylococcus|staphylococci]] and [[Streptococcus|streptococci]].&lt;ref name=Goodman/&gt; Ampicillin (originally branded as 'Penbritin') also demonstrated activity against Gram-negative organisms such as ''H. influenzae'', [[coliform]]s, and ''[[Proteus (bacterium)|Proteus]]'' spp.&lt;ref name = acred/&gt;
